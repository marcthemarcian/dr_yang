Heart Health Assessment

Assessing the risk of heart problems using 13 variables

Age

How old is the patient?

By the time you're middle-aged or older, enough plaque has built up to cause signs or symptoms. In men, the risk for heart disease increases after age 45. In women, the risk for heart disease increases after age 55.

What is the patient's sex?

Women generally have the same heart disease risk factors as men. However, some risk factors may affect women differently than men.Also, some risk factors, such as birth control pills and menopause, only affect women.

Resting Blood Pressure

How much is the person's resting blood pressure (in mmHg)?

Ideally, we should all have a blood pressure below 120 over 80 (120/80). This is the ideal blood pressure for people wishing to have good health. At this level, we have a much lower risk of heart disease or stroke. If your blood pressure is above 120/80mmHg, you will need to lower it.

Cholesterol Level

How much is the patient's serum cholesteral (in mg/dl)?

Your total serum cholesterol consists of high-density lipoprotein, or HDL cholesterol; low-density lipoprotein, or LDL cholesterol; and triglycerides. Levels are reported in milligrams per deciliter of blood, or mg/dL. The American Heart Association defines normal total cholesterol as less than 200 mg/dL.

Blood Sugar Level

Does the patient have a fasting blood sugar > 120 mg/dl?

Fasting, as the name suggests, means refraining from eating of drinking any liquids other than water for eight hours. A fasting blood sugar level less than 100 mg/dL (5.6 mmol/L) is normal. A fasting blood sugar level from 100 to 125 mg/dL (5.6 to 6.9 mmol/L) is considered prediabetes. If it's 126 mg/dL (7 mmol/L) or higher on two separate tests, you have diabetes.

Rest ECG

What is the patient's resting electrocardiographic results?

The electrocardiogram is a recording of the electrical activity of the heart as it undergoes excitation (depolarization) and recovery (polarization) to initiate each beat of the heart.

The normal pattern of the ECG allows analysis to determine whether there is any abnormality in any particular patient's ECG. The activity is classically represented by labeling the initial activity a P wave and in succession QRS, T and U waves. ST-segment elevation occurs when the injured muscle is located between normal muscle and the corresponding precordial electrode. Clues to left atrial hypertrophy include (1) P-wave duration greater than 0.11 s and notched P wave with an interpeak interval in excess of 0.04 s and (2) negative phase of P in V1 longer than 0.04 s and greater than 1 mm in lead VI.

Maximum Heart Rate

How much is the patient's maximum achieved heart rate?

The only way to determine your cycling maximum heart rate and maximum minute power is by using a properly constructed cycling test.

However such a test requires considerable motivation, physical stress and discomfort and it is inadvisable without medical clearance and supervision, particularly for individuals predisposed to coronary heart disease. We therefore suggest that people consider themselves “average” and use age predicted maximum heart rates despite the inaccuracy of such methods (as much as +/- 20 bpm).

Exercise-induced Angina

Does that patient experience exercise-induced angina?

Do you get chest discomfort with activity? This is known as exercise induced angina. Angina is generally caused from not getting enough blood through the arteries to supply the walls of the heart with enough blood flow to adequately pump.

ST depression

What is the patient's ST depression induced by exercise?

ST segment depression may be determined by measuring the vertical distance between the patient's trace and the isoelectric line at a location 2[3]-3 millimeters from the QRS complex. It is significant if it is more than 1 mm in V5-V6, or 1.5 mm in AVF or III.
